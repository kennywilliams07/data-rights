Just Some Basic Data Info

A lot of people don't think their data is a risk to their privacy. When you type something into a search engine, it doesn't just go away; it's collected and reciprocated into mined ads. Most people believe that your collected data goes to fueling a psychological change in society. Research shows that a data mining company based out of England had been employed by a presidential campaign to collect the data of Facebook users, regurgitate that data into personalized smear campaign ads against opponents to further win over voters.

Essentially the way that it basically breaks down, is that data is collected from your phone using an app- such as Facebook- and sent to a data center to be processed and mined, and then stored for further use. The company referenced above, Cambridge Analytica, for instance, bought data collected from FaceBook and used it to produce ads for the app (wonder why you've googled something and then seen it on Facebook or Instagram?). A professor in New York actually filed a suit to retrieve his data from the company and instead of just releasing it to him, it went to court and the company ultimately ended up doing a hard drive dump. The company is no longer in business due to multiple court cases involving the collection and processing of data.

Since Facebook was involved, that also led to the CEO and founder of the company, Mark Zuckerburg, being called into question by the US government. Questions were asked in regard to Facebook's data collection, selling of data, and whom they have sold data to. The multiple suits and court hearings have led to many people in the tech industry to call into question the people's data rights. Many movements have begun to fight for data rights, the ability to withdraw from data collection, and the usage of one's data.

What Happens To Your Data?

When you get on Facebook or Twitter, and see your friends posting which Marvel hero they align with or what this survey says their spirit animal is, you may think "Hey, I'd like to know if I'm Iron Man or Captain America." But what you don't think of, is will your answers be sent somewhere other than returned back to you as results? Leading up to the 2016 election, there began a large spike of targeted ads. People would notice that they would talk about something to a friend, or Google something and then see an ad for that very same thing on social media. That was just one thing; another was in the form of personality surveys. These personailty surveys were sent out by Cambridge Analytica (employeed by the Trump campaign).

If you were to take such surveys, your data would be collected to see what kind of person you are, and gear specific ads to tilt your voting. This tactic has been suspected to have been used in not just the 2016 election when the Trump campaign spent $1 million a day on social media marketing, but also the Brexit campaign, South American presidential elections, the Ted Cruz campaign, and many more around the world. The freaky part is that these surveys didn't just pull your data, but also the data of everyone on your friends list.

With the 21st century being the technological boom that it is, and evolving so rapidly, there aren't any regulations or laws protecting a person's data. Sounds scary, and it is. We don't even flinch when we download an app and just blindly accept the user agreements, allow access to camera or microphone, or use our locations. Is any of this information here to scare you? No. This information is here to bring awareness to Data Rights, which should be inate human rights. You should have the option to opt out of any data collection, have access to that data, and even use that data. Without data rights, the world will never see a true democracy. Our internet will become nothing more than a series of ads, fake news articles, and our bank accounts will be dry due to paying for ad-blockers and subscription-based VPNs.

Carole Cadwalladr is an investigative journalist for The Guardian and The Observer. She has spent hundreds of hours investigating the Cambridge Analytica company and has become a great source of information and advocate for human data rights.

David Carroll

David Carroll is a professor at the Parsons School of Design at The New School in New York City. He teaches media design and is one of the pioneers of human data rights. He was the first to file a suit against Cambridge Analytica to receive his data collection. Since then, he has become a centerpiece in the fight for data rights. He has begun teaching these fundamental rights to his students, among going to many hearings and protests.

Brittany Kaiser

Brittany Kaiser is a former employee of Cambridge Analytica who left prior to lawsuits and business ethics were called into question. Upon leaving, she has been known to be a whistle blower by exposing a lot of the offenses and practices of the company. She exposed CA on what exactly their process was for data collection, who they were employed by, and what they did with the people's data. She was issued a subpoena for association, but was not found guilty and acquitted of all charges.
